# Metaphor Ideas — for framing AI pedagogy

*A running list of candidate metaphors for how we talk about AI in design education.
Nothing chosen. The "grain of the machine" was **one** brainstorm idea and we've been
over-relying on it — this doc keeps the field open.*

**Where my head's at:** the most promising lane is probably **the design feedback
loop** — what's special about *design thinking* (iteration, critique, making-to-think,
diverge/converge) when applied to AI generally. Less "AI is a mysterious material,"
more "designers already have the right habits of mind for this." Section A is that
lane; the rest are alternatives and foils to think against.

**What we want from a metaphor (rough criteria):**
- Rings true to *designers* (uses studio habits they already have).
- Puts the student in the active seat, not the receiving seat.
- Not overdramatic — a working metaphor, not a manifesto.
- Generalizes beyond architecture (it's "AI pedagogy generally").
- Honest about limits (every metaphor distorts something).

---

## A. Design feedback-loop / design-thinking metaphors  ⟵ *the promising lane*

- **Reflective conversation with the material** (Schön)
  - *Illuminates:* design thinking *is* a back-and-forth with a situation that "talks back"; AI becomes another responsive material you think *through*, not a vending machine. Strong scholarly anchor (Donald Schön, *The Reflective Practitioner*).
  - *Distorts:* AI talks back fluently even when wrong — the material "lies" in a way clay doesn't.
  - *Pedagogy:* teach the move designers already know — propose, watch it respond, revise.

- **The desk crit / the crit loop**
  - *Illuminates:* working with AI as critique — you bring something, it responds, you judge the response. Students already know a crit isn't an order.
  - *Distorts:* a good critic has stakes and judgment; AI defaults to flattery.
  - *Pedagogy:* "treat its output like a crit, not a verdict."

- **The iteration spiral / sketch-critique-revise**
  - *Illuminates:* design is loops, not a straight line; AI changes the *speed and cost* of a loop, not its logic.
  - *Distorts:* faster loops can collapse the reflection that makes iteration valuable.
  - *Pedagogy:* the loop is the skill; AI just turns the crank faster — mind what you skip.

- **Diverge / converge (the double diamond)**
  - *Illuminates:* AI is great at *divergence* (volume of options) and seductive but dangerous at *convergence* (premature closure).
  - *Distorts:* implies a tidy two-phase process design rarely follows.
  - *Pedagogy:* use AI to widen, then do the narrowing yourself.

- **Trace paper / the overlay**
  - *Illuminates:* AI output as a fast, cheap, *disposable* sketch laid over your thinking — keep a line, toss the sheet.
  - *Distorts:* trace paper never pretends to be finished; renders do.
  - *Pedagogy:* generation is sketching, not production.

- **The fast prototype / something to react to**
  - *Illuminates:* the value isn't the artifact, it's having something concrete to push against; design thinks by making.
  - *Distorts:* a prototype you didn't build, you may not understand.
  - *Pedagogy:* make-to-think, then interrogate what you made.

## B. Material / craft metaphors  *(grain lives here — one option, not the headline)*

- **The grain of the machine** — AI as a material with a grain (dense vs. sparse training data); glides or tears. *Keep as one tool; stop treating it as the thesis.*
- **A new material we haven't learned yet** — like early concrete or CLT: powerful, unfamiliar, learned by testing. Less dramatic than "grain."
- **The lathe / potter's wheel** — a responsive medium you shape in a tight loop; skill is in the hands, not the machine.
- **Working "wet" (clay) vs. "dry" (stone)** — generative phases vs. committed ones.

## C. Collaboration / relationship metaphors

- **The brilliant intern** — fast, eager, widely-read, no judgment/accountability; you must check the work. (Common, a little tired, but legible.)
- **The studio peer / sounding board** — a collaborator to think out loud with; you still own the decision.
- **"Yes, and…" improv partner** — generative call-and-response; keeps ideas moving. Risk: it agrees too readily.
- **The translator** — moves between media/registers; every translation leaks, so you judge fidelity.

## D. Instrument / medium metaphors

- **An instrument you learn to play** — a violin, not a jukebox; output quality tracks the player's skill.
- **A medium you think in** (like drawing or modeling) — not a tool you use *on* a problem but one you reason *through*.
- **Power tool vs. hand tool** — faster, but removes you a step from the material; the offload trade-off made tangible.
- **Bicycle / exoskeleton for the mind** — amplifies effort but you still pedal. (Watch the over-optimism.)

## E. Foil / caution metaphors  *(useful for naming the risks)*

- **The mirror** — it reflects your framing back (sycophancy); ask leading questions, get flattering answers.
- **The current / the river** — convenient to drift with; design is steering against it when needed.
- **Autopilot** — fine on the cruise, dangerous in the moments that need a pilot; *who's flying?*
- **The well-paved cow path** — it takes you down the statistical average route unless you choose otherwise (anti-Pinterest).

## Combinations / threads to pull
- The strongest framing might be a **pair**: a *posture* metaphor (reflective conversation / the crit loop) + a *caution* metaphor (the mirror) — what to do, and what to watch.
- "Design thinking is already AI-ready" as the talk's spine: designers iterate, critique, prototype, and tolerate ambiguity — exactly the habits AI rewards. The metaphor just names that.
- Test any finalist against: does it survive outside architecture (writing, science, business)? The lecture audience is broader than designers.
