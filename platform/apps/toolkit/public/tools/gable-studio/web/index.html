How to use this data — testing your design decisions

These read-outs are transparent proxies: simple, deterministic formulas — the same maths runs here and in the exported Rhino/Grasshopper script. Read them as relative signals for comparing options, not certified numbers. For validated results use Ladybug Tools / EnergyPlus (energy), Radiance (daylight) or CFD (wind).

The design loop
  1. Shape the massing with the building & terrain controls.
  2. Read the performance data move as you drag.
  3. Constrain — write your intent as testable rules.
  4. Score — watch the pass/fail dots and the % score.
  5. Export to Rhino 8 + Grasshopper, build it, then re-import the .3dm and re-test against the same rules.
Test one decision at a time

Treat each move as an A/B test: note a metric, change a single control (say roof pitch or the north angle), and compare. The size of the jump tells you how much leverage that variable has over that goal — a flat response means it isn't the lever you thought it was.

Turn intent into rules

A rule is a claim you can check, e.g. winter useful sun > 3 or overheat ratio < 1. Soft rules add to the score by their weight (say which goals matter most); hard rules must pass for the design to count. Use between / outside for comfort bands. The score is the weighted fraction of soft rules met — one defensible number for “does this massing do what I said it should?”

See where, not just how much

Analysis view colours the envelope blue → red (low → high), so you can see where the sun or wind actually lands — the shaded band under the eaves, the windward wall — not just a total. Switch the overlay (sun-now / yearly / wind) and hover any metric for a plain-English definition.

Reading the families
  • Solar — want winter-useful high and overheat ratio below 1.
  • Wind — exposure & pressure are loads to resist (pressure grows with speed²).
  • Air — stack index/height is the strength of buoyancy-driven natural ventilation (higher = stronger).
  • View — openness toward your target azimuth and to the sky.
  • Earth — soil contact, thermal mass and buried fraction (earth-coupling).
  • Form — volume, glazing ratio and pitch: the geometry behind the rest.
Know the limits

The proxies ignore diffuse/cloud sky, glazing transmittance, fine self-shading, true pressure coefficients and transient heat flow. They’re built for early form-finding and teaching — to rank options and pressure-test intent — not for code compliance.

Where the analysis comes from

These are transparent teaching proxies, not validated simulation. Each metric is a pure, deterministic function of the parameters — the same formula runs in the browser and in the exported Rhino/Grasshopper python (a parity test checks they agree). For certified analysis use Ladybug Tools / EnergyPlus (energy), Radiance (daylight), or CFD (wind).

Units: inputs and metrics are shown in feet (ft², ft³, mph) for convenience; the proxies and the exported Rhino/GH python compute in metres (SI), so the formulas below are SI and values are converted only for display.

Frame & sun position

+X East, +Y North, +Z Up; azimuth clockwise from North. Sun is sampled at 3 declinations (δ = +23.45° summer, 0° equinox, −23.45° winter) × 5 hours (08–16). Altitude: sin α = sin φ·sin δ + cos φ·cos δ·cos H; azimuth: cos A = (sin δ − sin φ·sin α)/(cos φ·cos α), with hour angle H = 15°·(hour−12). Standard solar geometry — Duffie & Beckman, “Solar Engineering of Thermal Processes”; NOAA solar-position equations.

Solar gain idx

Direct-beam incidence proxy: Σ area · max(0, n·L) over the sun samples (Lambert cosine law), summed over the whole envelope and, separately, over the glazed apertures (“useful” gain), split by season and orientation. Ignores diffuse/cloud, glazing transmittance, and self-shading — so read it as relative, not W.

Wind exposure & load m² · N

Windward projected area Σ A · max(0, n·f) (f = wind-from direction). Load proxy = dynamic pressure q = ½·ρ·V² (ρ = 1.225 kg/m³) × exposure. ASHRAE / Eurocode EN 1991-1-4 dynamic pressure; true pressure coefficients need wind-tunnel/CFD.

Wind channelling ×

Plan “throat” ratio between the walls and the plinth across the wind (continuity/venturi heuristic): wider building ÷ narrowest side gap. A massing cue, not CFD.

Stack ventilation idx · m

Buoyancy-driven flow Q = Cd · A* · √(2g·Δz·ΔT/T), effective opening area A* = 1/√(1/A_in² + 1/A_out²), with low apertures as inlets and high ones (incl. the roof) as outlets; Cd = 0.61, T = 293 K. CIBSE AM10 “Natural ventilation in non-domestic buildings”; ASHRAE Fundamentals.

Views sr

Projected solid angle of each aperture from an interior eye point, Ω ≈ A·cos θ / r²; “view toward target” weights Ω by alignment to a chosen azimuth; “sky view” uses the roof aperture upward. Solid-angle / view-factor geometry.

Earth coupling & thermal mass m² · MJ/K·m³

Soil-contact area = plinth faces below the terrain height; thermal-mass ratio = (slab volume × ≈2.0 MJ/m³·K for concrete) ÷ enclosed volume; buried fraction = slab side below grade ÷ thickness. Concrete volumetric heat capacity ≈ 2.0 MJ/m³·K, CIBSE Guide A; steady-geometry proxy, not transient heat-flow.

Terrain

The ravine is procedural: a smoothstep drop across an edge plus gentle sinusoidal undulation (Perlin smoothstep t²(3−2t)) — illustrative ground, not survey data.

Location
