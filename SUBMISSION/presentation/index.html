01 · The course & the problem

Students can't yet justify their designs.

  • In a fundamentals studio, students lack the sophistication to justify their designs — through site environmental forces, historical precedent, or creative form generation.
  • Our job, as always, is to level up their reasoning, synthesis, and analysis of both a real-world site and the invented building placed on it.
  • What's new: agentically-coded AI tools give them logics, language, and data — and put computational analysis (solar gain, wind, thermal mass, views) within reach for the first time.

02 · The redesign & where AI fits

Use sophisticated tools — and build simpler ones together.

Use

I demo how I vibe-code tools of a more sophisticated caliber and give students access — the heavy computational lifting that used to need expensive software or skills they don't have yet.

Build

We vibe-code simpler tools together in class. Students aren't only users — they see how the tools are made, and make some themselves.

The stance threaded through both: trust but verify — embrace the tools and iterate often, but push to find edge-cases and failure modes, brainstorming in a fluid but skeptical manner.
Student = the designer in charge, who verifies.   AI = a fast instrument for logic, language, data & analysis — checked, not obeyed.

03 · Outcome & 04 · how I'll measure it
Students justify the design — and the process that produced it — with more sophisticated reasoning, synthesis, and analysis, while staying the designer who verifies and defends the work.

Collect, across milestones

  • Design justifications — verbal at pin-ups + written
  • What they accepted / changed / rejected from AI, and why
  • The edge-cases & failure modes they caught

How I'll know it's working

  • A rubric on sophistication of justification + genuineness of verification
  • Compare early → late: sharper reasoning, realer skepticism over the term

05 · The challenge I haven't solved

"Trust but verify" is easy to say — and easy to fake.

  • A novice can slide two ways: accept the AI's confident output uncritically, or perform skepticism without really hunting edge-cases and failure modes.
  • Police the verifying too hard and it becomes a punitive tax — killing the curious, genuine version right along with the fake one.
  • Stakes: students gain pro-level reach but not the judgment to wield it — sophistication without justification, the very gap I set out to close.
