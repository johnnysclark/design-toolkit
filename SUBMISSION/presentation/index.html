01 · The course & the problem

AI is most dangerous for the novice.

  • My second-years already use AI — and can't yet see when its confident, polished output is wrong. A hallucinated precedent reads exactly like a real one.
  • Studio has always graded the artifact. When AI makes a finished-looking board in seconds, grading the artifact rewards polish over judgment — and can't tell owned work from offloaded work.
  • The risk isn't cheating. It's novices learning the wrong lesson at the worst time: polish = architecture, AI = oracle.

03 · The learning outcome
A second-year can produce and defend a legible trace of their process — where AI entered, what they trusted and why, what they verified, and what they changed — so reasoning is visible and ownable, no matter how finished the artifact looks.
Process literacy. Not "use AI well." Not "make a good building." The capacity underneath both.

04 · My measurement approach

The tools emit the evidence.

Collect (byproducts, no busywork)

  • Four-line provenance logs
  • Verification worksheets (✓ / ? / ⚠)
  • Override events — rejected/changed + the reason
  • Trust calibration — tags vs. what proved true
  • "% in your words" · unaided re-derivation checks

05 · The challenge I haven't solved

Grade the trace, and students learn to perform the trace.

  • A clean log and a staged "critique of the AI" can be faked — and novices are expert at telling teachers what they want to see.
  • Police it harder and the trace becomes a punitive tax that kills the honest version right alongside the fake one.
  • Stakes: "grade the trace" silently becomes "grade the performance of a trace." Offloading, laundered through good-looking logs — worse than where I started.

My question for the cohort
How do you tell genuine reflection from performed reflection — without policing the documentation so hard it becomes punitive and kills the honest version too?
