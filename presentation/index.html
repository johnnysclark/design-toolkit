01 — The course & the problem

The course

Architecture 273 — Fundamentals of Design. Students carry a building from a real site through concept, form, and representation — then stand up and defend it.

It's where architects are made: not by producing a board, but by building judgment and learning to argue a decision under questioning.

The problem

My beginners can design. They can't yet justify the design — they reach for taste and habit, not evidence.

The three places an argument is won — site forces, precedent, form — are exactly where a beginner is weakest. My job is to level up their reasoning, synthesis, and analysis.

The gap isn't talent. It's justification.
02 — The redesign

Design thinking is already AI-ready.

Don't treat AI as an oracle. Bring the design feedback loop to it — because the studio loop is the agent loop.

scheme→ pin-up→ crit→ revise ≡ propose→ act→ observe→ revise

The opportunity: agentic tools hand a beginner the logics, language, and data — and computational analysis (sun, wind, thermal, views) out of reach until this year. The stance: embrace them fast, trust but verify — fluid but skeptical, hunting the edge cases. That's how you build a resilient designer.

04 — The learning outcome

The outcome studio always wanted: students who can justify the work — the final design and the process behind it.

The new muscle that gets them there — calibrated skepticism: trust in AI, priced against evidence, while staying the author of every decision.

not credulousaccepting everything the model says
not paralyzedrefusing to use it at all
but calibratedtrust but verify, continuously, against evidence

It's the crit — architecture's native skill — pointed at the machine.

05 — How I'll measure it

Grade the trace, not just the output.

  • 1·Four-line provenance log — tool / asked / kept–changed–rejected / verified.
  • 2·Override ledger — every time the student overruled the model, and why. An empty log is a red flag.
  • 3·Verification worksheet — did the student's verdict agree with the tag? (calibration, not just facts)
  • 4·Edge-case log + "% in your words" — did they find where it breaks? And is the writing theirs, not ghostwritten?
provenance.log
tool: Precedent / Research (grounded)
asked: naturally-ventilated libraries, hot-humid
kept/changed/rejected: rejected "33% energy cut" — no source
verified: 2 of 5 claims ✓ via journal DOI

Using the tool produces the assessment artifact for free — and over the term, catch-rate should rise.

06 — The challenge I haven't solved

The students who most need the reach are the least able to verify it.

The verification paradox. "Trust but verify" needs the expertise to know when to distrust — the very thing a beginner lacks. Push "embrace" and they offload judgment; push "verify" and I've slowed the tool that was meant to extend them.
If I get it wrong: students perform a trace instead of living it — overrides faked, logs tidied. Polish stands in for evidence again, now one level up, in the documentation of the thinking.

Riding alongside: equity (the graded path must run on free / approved tools) and the novice (most exposed to invisible error).

my question — for the cohort

I'm asking beginners to embrace powerful tools fast and verify them well — but verification is the expertise they don't have yet. How do you teach novices to verify what they can't fully judge — and grade the process without teaching them to fake it?

What, in a trace, tells real trust-calibration apart from performed compliance?
